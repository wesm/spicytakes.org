I've known Tobi for over twenty years now. Right from the earliest days of Ruby on Rails, when he was building Snowdevil, which eventually became
[Shopify](https://shopify.com)
, to sell snowboards online. Here's
[his first commit to Rails](https://github.com/rails/rails/commit/a544d7aa372a080b77940571a3a169496045670e)
from 2004, which improved the ergonomics of controller testing. Just one out of
[the 131 commits he made to the framework from 2004-2010](https://contributors.rubyonrails.org/contributors/tobias-lutke/commits)
-- a record still good enough to be in
[the top 100 all-time contributors to Rails](https://contributors.rubyonrails.org/)
!
But Tobi's contributions to Ruby on Rails extend far beyond his individual commits to the framework, creating
[Active Merchant](https://activemerchant.org/)
and the Liquid templating system, or
[serving on the Rails Core Team](https://rubyonrails.org/community#alumni)
back in the early days. With Shopify, Tobi more or less single-handedly
[killed the zombie argument that Rails couldn't scale](https://x.com/tobi/status/1728524453854756883)
by building the world's most popular hosted e-commerce platform and routing a sizable portion of all online sales through it.
In the process, Tobi built an incredible technical organization to support this effort. Shopify employs a third of the Rails Core Team, developed the YJIT compiler for Ruby, and contributed in a billion other ways. They are without a doubt the most generous benefactor in the Ruby on Rails world.
So when Tobi asked me whether I'd be interested in joining Shopify's board, I needed no pause to consider the invitation.
[OF COURSE I WOULD!](https://www.shopify.com/news/david-heinemeier-hansson-board)
But to be honest, it wasn't just a reflexive answer to service the gratitude I've felt toward Shopify for many years. It was also to satisfy a selfish curiosity to wrestle with problems at a scale that none of my own work has ever touched.
Both in terms of the frontier programming problems inherent in dealing with a
[majestic monolith](https://signalvnoise.com/svn3/the-majestic-monolith/)
clocking in at five million lines of code, and the challenge of guiding thousands of programmers to productively extend it, Shopify deals with a scale several orders of magnitude beyond what I do day-to-day at 37signals. That's interesting!
So too is the sheer magnitude of the impact Shopify is having on the world of commerce. While much of the web is decaying to
[enshitification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enshittification)
and entropy, Shopify stores stand out by being faster to browse, quicker to checkout, and easier to trust. That's enabling a vast array of individual entrepreneurs and businesses to have a competitive shopping experience against the likes of Amazon, without needing huge teams to do it.
It's always a delight when I
[find](https://akkogear.eu/)
[a](https://www.lofree.co/)
[cool](https://www.aviatornation.com/)
[store](https://us.store.bambulab.com/)
, and I learn that it's running on Shopify. As I spoke with Tobi about on
[the announcement show](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uAgHlSLxcTU&t=617s)
, this was really hammered home after I got into mechanical keyboards. Seemingly every single vendor of thocky and clicky keyboards use Shopify! And when I see that, not only am I sure that buying won't be a hassle, but I also know I'm not going to get scammed. That's the Shopify magic: Leveling the commercial playing field between some obscure keyboard maker and the consolidated titans of e-commerce.
And now I get to help further that mission from the inside! What a treat. Thanks Tobi!
