So all messages are actually signed so that you don't have people with the URL of your controller don't actually have the ability to run code. And then we also will help out with SSH tunnels. IPython will actually launch SSH tunnels for you if need be.

**Audience member:** I remember multiprocessing had a problem pickling a bunch of different objects. How do you send your code around? Do you get around those problems?

**Audience member:** In your experience, how robust is it all? How often do you have to just reset the universe?

**Min Ragan-Kelley:** So the standard model is that an IPython cluster lasts for the lifetime of a simulation or a workload. So rather than with PBS, where you start it once and it runs for the lifetime of your hardware, we expect most IPython clusters to run for closer to the order of hours, days, or maybe a week or two. And I haven't had too much need for killing it and restarting it. Right now, with the current code, the controller is a single point of failure. If your controller goes down, then your engines are useless. There's a currently outstanding pull request that allows the controller to die and come back and the engines continue to function.

It is fairly tolerant of engines going horribly wrong. Engines segfault all over the place and the load balancer says, oh, that engine's gone, I'll resubmit work somewhere else. For engines, it's very tolerant of engines just exploding all over the place. For the controller, that's not true.

**Audience member:** What's the recommended procedure for shutting down a cluster when you're done with it?

**Min Ragan-Kelley:** rc.shutdown. By default, shutdown shuts down all your engines. So the cluster process was running, waiting for the cluster to exit, and I just called shutdown. It brought down the engines, and it brought down the controller. And you can shut down individual engines one at a time.

**Audience member:** When you call shutdown, what if there are some engines which have messages still pending?

**Min Ragan-Kelley:** It's basically trusting you. If you say shutdown, it believes you mean it, and it believes you mean it right now. It will finish its current computation, and then exit cleanly. But it will not compute anything currently outstanding in its queue.

**Audience member:** Is there a polite shutdown, saying shutdown after everything is done?

**Min Ragan-Kelley:** What I would do is from a client script, because you can wait on work, whether you submitted it or not. You can say, wait for the currently running tasks, and then submit shutdown. So I would basically do polite shutdown at the client level.
