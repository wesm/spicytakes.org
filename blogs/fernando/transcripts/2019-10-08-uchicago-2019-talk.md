### The Perverse Incentives of "Novel and Transformative"

Scientific open source has largely happened despite official support from the structures that give us support. Unfortunately, a lot of the funding that goes into scientific software infrastructure suffers from this perverse vice of go build the new shiny thing, rather than work with anything that actually gets the job done. It's not about getting the job done, it's about the new shiny thing.

There's two keywords that those of you who haven't yet had the pleasure of writing an NSF grant will eventually become very familiar with. They are the word "novel" and the word "transformative." Those two stupid words are the most poisonous piece of information that has ever been injected into the federal funding system because they justify this kind of stuff happening over and over, and literally billions of dollars have been sacrificed on the altar of those two stupid words.

The Pangeo project is an exception. They said, we're not doing that. The road almost gets us there. We're just going to finish, add the last bit of pavement. We're going to do it, and then we're going to straighten a couple of the curves that are a little bit ugly, and we'll do it for a fraction of the cost, and everybody will be getting there tomorrow. That's exactly what they did. I did say this to the NSF a few weeks ago, and they were receptive. I think the problem is that the "novel and transformative" nonsense is courtesy of Congress, so I think that one's hard to remove.

### The Incentive Misalignment Grid

This is the problem. On any dimension that you look at, the creation and construction of these open, collaborative tools and ideas is at odds with the incentive mechanisms that we have throughout the system, from academic hiring and promotion and publishing and tenure to grant writing to awards. On each of these dimensions, I think this model can be healthier and more productive and more effective and deliver actually more value both to individuals and to society, but we've managed to engineer a system of incentives that makes exactly the right things the hardest things to do, and that's a problem.

### The Domain-Agnostic Backbone: Nobody's Problem

I want to concentrate on the fact that things are getting better. In particular over the years we've built all of this stack that takes Python and then has general purpose tools like NumPy and IPython and SciPy and Matplotlib, a little bit more specialized ones but still broad spectrum tools like scikit-learn or scikit-image, and all the way to very domain specific things like SunPy for solar physics.

Of those domain-specific tools there are many, many more. In the domains obviously you have as many tools as there are disciplinary endeavors. Ignoring Python for a minute, these two rings of what I call the domain-agnostic backbone, the somewhat generic tools, that's the space where I spend most of my time, and it's not real computer science, it's not real research, it's basically nobody's problem. That stuff is nobody's problem. It's the stuff that gives no one credit, that gives no one a career, that no one wants to fund. Guess what? You can't build the rest without it. You can't build the rest without it, and it's very tricky to find ways of justifying.

I'm being obviously for theatrical effect a bit exaggerated. We have gotten funding. We have gotten grants. I do have a job. It's not all doom and gloom. But it has been very hard. I got my PhD in 2002, and I got my job in 2017. 15 years is not exactly a career plan that anyone should realistically consider.

### Organizations Trying to Help

This is not a new problem. There's a bunch of organizations trying to help, in the direction of teaching people across some of the skills that are needed to do this work better, whether it's in software or data management. The Carpentries is a fantastic organization that teaches short focused workshops.

In terms of what are the realistic career paths for people with a scientific training but who would like to spend their time closer to the code because that's what they enjoy doing, there's a movement called the Research Software Engineering career path that really began in the UK and is very well organized there. Now, there are people being hired as RSEs. Even recently, there was the first faculty hire, Simon Hettrick, who got a faculty position at U Southampton, fully tenured as an RSE, and they hired him to say, you should come as a research software engineer professor. This is beginning to change. They recently opened up a US chapter.

In 2012, actually with John Hunter and Travis Oliphant and a couple of other people, five of us co-founded an organization that now is led by much better people than us and has grown a lot called NumFOCUS, which is an actual nonprofit 501(c)(3) that helps bridge between these distributed open source communities, the academic world and the industry world, and provides things like legal services and fiscal sponsorship and also a bunch of other really interesting initiatives in diversity and better community management for a lot of the scientifically focused packages. It's not just about free software in general, which is a perfectly fine thing to worry about. That's a separate problem. It's about open source software in science.

Mozilla, the people who make Firefox, they actually run really interesting efforts around organizational leadership and management, and that is extremely valuable. We've had folks from Jupyter participate in some of their trainings. They're a very interesting organization that is working on the problem of leadership in open heterogeneous communities.

The problem of getting academic credit for things like open source software or open source educational content is a really important one. There are two folks in our community, Lorena Barba, who's faculty at George Washington, and Arfon Smith, who is at the Space Telescope at Johns Hopkins, who both lead the Journal of Open Source Software and the Journal of Open Source Education, which allow you to get effectively academic credit for software or educational artifacts that have been developed in the open, but in the atomic unit that people know how to count in a more academic context, with minimal effort. It's actually a really well thought out model.

### Hack Weeks: Connecting Training to Research

This notion of connecting training, but now not in a bootcampy sense, but actually to the research questions of a discipline, and building a community that develops ideas around research problems in a discipline while learning how to build, is something where a pattern emerged called Hack Weeks. It began with Astro Hack Week, but now there's Geo Hack Week, Ocean Hack Week, ICESat Hack Week, the neuroscience one got big enough that now it's called Neuro Hack Academy because it lasts two weeks.

The idea of these events is to connect a group of people from undergrads to senior PIs with a lot of attention paid to how to engineer that community to work for a week or two on both learning skills and actually hacking on things, building new projects, testing new ideas that may lead to papers, to new research projects, to collaborations, to new tools being developed. It has been a phenomenally successful model. The team that leads these actually wrote a paper in PNAS that I encourage anyone interested in education and community to read. It's a really well thought out reflection on how the human and social engineering behind these things needs to be done.

For example, the ICESat-2 one was phenomenal. I participated, I taught in it, and it was so much fun that I got together with the organizers and we just put together, last week, a new NASA grant proposal on developing open source Python tools for analyzing data from ICESat-2, which is a LIDAR instrument that NASA just sent up to monitor ice on Earth, polar regions, mountain ice, et cetera.

### Catastrophic Success

Fundamentally, even though these organizations exist and I wanted to give credit to their efforts, what we have is an economic problem, an organizational problem that isn't fully solved. We're victims of what Tracy Teal from the Carpentries very aptly calls "catastrophic success."

A paper came out a few years ago looking at the role of IDL, which was the most heavily used proprietary tool in astronomy, compared to Python, and by 2015, Python was by and large basically wiping IDL out of existence. Well, it turns out that IDL or Mathematica or MATLAB are companies that employ thousands of people. Those thousands of people actually did a job. They did useful things like writing good code and documenting it and fixing bugs in it. Yes, it was closed source. Yes, it was proprietary. But they weren't just sitting there collecting rent for nothing.

Who is going to do that work? If we are saying that the scientific backbone of Event Horizon Telescope, of LIGO, of every project funded by NSF, DOE, DOD, NIH, et cetera, is all going to be done on open source software, where's the work of those people? Who's paying for that work? Who's going to sustain all that?

### Strategic Risk and the 0.1% Argument

This is a problem of strategic risk. If you want to have a seat at the table of managing that risk, you have to invest. You have to put resources into the problem, because if not, that space is going to get filled up strictly by the commercial interests who do see value in these things, who are actually developing also open source tools, but open source tools as a strategic control asset. TensorFlow, PyTorch, et cetera, are tools of the strategic battle around machine learning on the planet from the richest companies on earth, and as engineering recruitment tools. They're not building those tools for the greater good of an open multi-stakeholder interoperable community. They're building those things for themselves. If we want a voice in the development of these things, the scientific community and the funders of the scientific community have to actually invest, because you get a voice at the table by participating, not simply by saying that you should have a voice.

I think there's a lot of bang to be had for the buck here. The federal R&D budget is of the order of $200 billion, give or take. A tenth of a percent of that is $200 million per year. A tenth of a percent. Do you think 0.1% of research today involves computers, or maybe a little bit more than 0.1%? How much of research involves computers today in an important way? 30%? 50%? 70%? I don't know, but a lot. It's not 0.1%. If we devoted 0.1%, we'd be spending $200 million on core open source scientific software infrastructure. That would already go a long way.

But the trick is we have to spend it well. This is an interesting problem. I don't have all the answers. I have some ideas. I've thought a lot about it. It's an interesting problem of organizations. It's an interesting problem of incentives. We need to recognize that open source projects are real, complex human organizations that live in a very interesting intersection of spaces and interests. Understand what are the ones that work well. What do they do well? What have they done? How can we nurture them? How can we nurture that without killing creativity, without killing innovation? How can we do it in a way that is actually more inclusive?

Guess what? The notion that volunteers are going to do this job is a great way to make sure that only people who can afford to volunteer do the job, which means typically less women and minorities, because they tend not to have the freedom of spending their nights and weekends doing this, because maybe they have to have a second job. Maybe they have to take care of their families. If you want to actually bring other people to the table, you have to recognize that this is a job.

### Open Source Infrastructure as a Systemic Problem

This is not just us. I had a quick look, and in the last week, four different blog posts attacking different angles of this question very thoughtfully have been posted. These are mostly about the web and industry side of open source, a little bit less about science, but a lot of the same questions and reflections apply.

This quote from the bottom is from Matthew Garrett. He's an old Linux contributor, very core Linux guy, but that statement is exactly what we've been talking about. These projects that are critical to the most important scientific results of the day are chronically underfunded and have effectively no sustainability path.

I recommend anyone interested in this that you read this report. It was written by Nadia Eghbal. She's an engineer at Protocol Labs. This was paid for by the Ford Foundation. It's a really well done report on thinking about open source as infrastructure. In her case, again, more on the industry than the science side, but it's a brilliant piece of background research on this topic. Nadia has been thinking a lot about how to tackle this problem creatively, referring to work from people like Elinor Ostrom, Nobel Prize winner, who did a ton of work on studying how sustainable communities govern common resources. A lot of her work was done around water resource management and watersheds in California in the 70s. I think she died in 2009 or so, but her work is one of the foundational pieces of theory around this.

**Q:** I was just wondering if you could say a little bit about what were the keys to the success of IPython and Jupyter in terms of adoption. Like you said, you don't just put out a GitHub repo and things take off. What are the key things that you need to do?

**A:** Obviously, this is a highly biased take from my own history, but I think a few factors played a role. One was we were really using it. We weren't building something just on some external mandate. At the beginning, it was me, but quickly others. The people, and if we look back at the archives, the people on the IPython mailing list from early on were working scientists, were adding features, sending feedback. It was kind of the eat your own dog food from day one. And that was true. John needed Matplotlib for his work. Travis was working on the predecessor to NumPy and SciPy as a medical imaging postdoc. Eric Jones had founded a company to sell scientific Python tools to industry. There was this notion that we were building something to solve a real problem.

Second, there was something about a community that was very willing to collaborate. To give you an example, we met in 2005 because of that neuroscience thing, and we got together at Berkeley, a few of us, John and Eric and Travis and I and a few others, and we realized: look, IPython is about interactive computing but it has a bunch of numerical utilities and a bunch of plotting stuff. SciPy is an algorithms toolbox that has a visualization component and a shell. Matplotlib is a plotting library that has a shell and numerical utilities. Let's just diagonalize this matrix. This confusion matrix is leaking. Let's diagonalize this thing. IPython does interactivity, SciPy does algorithms, and Matplotlib does plotting.

And we were willing to just move, literally move code around and share it. There was a sense of trust and willingness. In fact, IPython early on was with the GPL. That's what I had done. And John said, hey, as long as you're GPL, we can't copy and paste. We're not allowed to copy and paste. All right, let's just change that license so that literally we can move the code into the right places. There was no sense of we need to fight for credit here. There was a sense of what's the right thing to do, and let's just do the right thing.

And then I think there was the luck of good timing. The web was growing. The tools for collaboration and open source were growing. And the desire for open tools was growing. We kind of rode a good wave of timing. I think the combination of doing a few of these things well and riding a good wave of timing helped a lot.
