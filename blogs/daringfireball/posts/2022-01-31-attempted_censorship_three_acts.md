**Act 1:** The New York Times, last week: “School Board in Tennessee Bans Teaching of Holocaust Novel ‘Maus’”:


> A school board in Tennessee voted unanimously this month to ban
> “Maus,” a Pulitzer Prize-winning graphic novel about the
> Holocaust, from being taught in its classrooms because the book
> contains material that board members said was inappropriate for
> students.
> According to minutes of its meeting, the 10-person board, in
> McMinn County, Tenn., voted on Jan. 10 to remove the book from the
> eighth-grade curriculum. Members of the board said the book, which
> [portrays Jews as mice and Nazis as cats](https://www.nytimes.com/2011/12/04/books/review/the-making-of-maus.html) in recounting the
> author’s parents’ experience during the Holocaust, contained
> inappropriate curse words and a depiction of a naked character.


**Act 2:** [Stephen King, in a keynote address on school censorship back in 1996](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=T-rrg2MRUGA) (35:00):


> Push them a little further and they’ll invoke “family values”, a
> phrase that more and more frequently makes me feel like falling to
> the floor and projectile vomiting. Censorship and the suppression
> of reading materials is rarely about family values and almost
> always about control. Who is snapping the whip, who is saying
> “no”, and who is saying “go”. Censorship’s bottom line is this: if
> the novel *Christine* offends *me*, I don’t want to just make sure
> it’s kept from my kid, I want to make sure it’s kept from *your*
> kid as well. And all the kids.
> This bit of intellectual arrogance — undemocratic and as old as
> time — is best expressed this way: *If it’s bad for me and my
> family, it’s bad for everyone’s family*. Yet when books are run
> out of school classrooms and even out of school libraries as a
> result of this idea, I’m never much disturbed. Not as a citizen,
> not as a writer, not even as a schoolteacher, which I was trained
> to be and used to do. What I tell the kids is don’t get mad, get
> even. Don’t spend time waving signs or carrying petitions around
> the neighborhood. Instead run — don’t walk — to the nearest
> non-school library or to the local bookstore, and get whatever it
> was they banned. Read what they’re trying to keep out of your
> eyes. Read what they’re trying to keep out of your brain. Because
> that’s exactly what you need to know.


**Act 3:** Today: “[‘Maus’ Tops Bestseller Lists After Tennessee School District Yanks It From Curriculum](https://talkingpointsmemo.com/news/maus-art-spiegelman-amazon-bestseller-list)”:


> As of Monday morning, *The Complete Maus* held the No. 2 spot
> among Amazon’s best sellers in books. *Maus I*, an earlier
> published book that is the first part of *The Complete Maus*, was
> also the No. 3 best-selling book on Amazon.
> In the category of comics and graphic novels, four editions of
> *Maus*, in hardcover and paperback versions, were in the top five
> bestsellers.
