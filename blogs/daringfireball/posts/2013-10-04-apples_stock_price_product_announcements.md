Halfway through his otherwise excellent piece today for The New York Times [on the introduction of the original iPhone in 2007](http://www.nytimes.com/2013/10/06/magazine/and-then-steve-said-let-there-be-an-iphone.html?smid=tw-share&_r=0&pagewanted=all), Fred Vogelstein interrupts the narrative to insert a cookie-cutter bit about Apple being lost without Steve Jobs at the helm. It includes this:


> When Jobs ran Apple, the company was an innovation machine,
> churning out revolutionary products every three to five years. He
> told his biographer, Walter Isaacson, that he had another
> breakthrough coming — a revolution in TV. But under Cook, nothing
> has materialized, and the lack of confidence among investors is
> palpable. Apple product announcements used to routinely send its
> stock soaring. When Cook presented the latest smartphones in
> September, the iPhone 5c and the iPhone 5s, Apple’s stock *fell*
> 10 percent.


Leave aside for now the fact that Tim Cook has been CEO for only two years and that Vogelstein himself acknowledges that under Jobs, the revolutionary products came “every three to five years”. The stuff about the stock price is just nonsense — Apple’s stock price routinely fell after products announcements by Steve Jobs. For all the lip service paid to “innovation”, Wall Street tends to be conservative, rewarding the conventional and punishing the unconventional.


You can actually check these things. Stock prices and the dates of Steve Jobs’s product announcements are matters of fact:

- 23 October 2001, introduction of original iPod: [AAPL fell about 5 percent](http://finance.yahoo.com/q/hp?s=AAPL&a=09&b=22&c=2001&d=09&e=24&f=2001&g=d).
- 7 January 2002, Macworld Expo keynote: [AAPL fell 4 percent](http://finance.yahoo.com/q/hp?s=AAPL&a=00&b=7&c=2002&d=00&e=8&f=2002&g=d).
- 7 January 2003, Macworld Expo keynote: [AAPL fell slightly](http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=aapl+january+6-9+2003).
- 6 January 2004, Macworld Expo keynote, introduction of iPod Mini: [AAPL fell slightly](http://finance.yahoo.com/q/hp?s=AAPL&a=00&b=6&c=2004&d=00&e=7&f=2004&g=d).
- 11 January 2005, Macworld Expo keynote: [AAPL fell over 6 percent](http://finance.yahoo.com/q/hp?s=AAPL&a=00&b=10&c=2005&d=00&e=12&f=2005&g=d).
- 10 January 2006, Macworld Expo keynote: [AAPL rose over 6 percent](http://finance.yahoo.com/q/hp?s=AAPL&a=00&b=9&c=2006&d=00&e=11&f=2006&g=d).
- 9 January 2007, Macworld Expo keynote, introduction of the iPhone, now seen as the biggest and most important product introduction in Apple, and perhaps industry, history: [AAPL rose over 8 percent](http://finance.yahoo.com/q/hp?s=AAPL&a=00&b=8&c=2007&d=00&e=10&f=2007&g=d).
- 15 January 2008, Macworld Expo keynote, introduction of MacBook Air: [AAPL fell over 5 percent](http://finance.yahoo.com/q/hp?s=AAPL&a=00&b=14&c=2008&d=00&e=16&f=2008&g=d).
- 9 June 2008, WWDC keynote, introduction of iPhone 3G: [AAPL fell 2 percent](http://finance.yahoo.com/q/hp?s=AAPL&a=05&b=6&c=2008&d=05&e=10&f=2008&g=d)
- 27 January 2010, introduction of original iPad: [AAPL was up slightly on the day, but then dropped and kept dropping for days](http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=aapl+january+26-29+2010).
- 7 June 2010, introduction of iPhone 4 (last phone introduced by Jobs): [AAPL fell slightly, then dropped 3 percent the next day](http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=aapl+january+26-29+2010).


The 10 percent drop in Apple’s share price on the day of the 5S/5C introduction is certainly unusual, but everything about Apple’s share price the last few years has been unusual, particularly its volatility. Wall Street does not understand the iPhone. But it’s wrong, factually and provably wrong, to claim that “Apple product announcements used to routinely send its stock soaring.” If anything, the *opposite* has been true: Apple stock has routinely fallen after product announcements, including the entire final decade of Steve Jobs’s career.
