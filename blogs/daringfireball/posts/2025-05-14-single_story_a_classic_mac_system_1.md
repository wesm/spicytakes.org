Following up on [my gripe regarding the alternative *a* glyph](https://daringfireball.net/linked/2025/05/12/that-stupid-apple-notes-a) used in Apple Notes, here’s [Kevin Fox, tweeting on Threads](https://www.threads.com/@kfury/post/DJlFkNfR5ik):


> While we’re waxing nostalgic on the Original Mac, a Daring
> Fireball post today (below) reminded me of another piece of Mac
> 128k trivia.
> Until shortly before the official release, the ‘a’ in Geneva was a
> single story ‘a’ like you see currently (and to some,
> infuriatingly) in the Notes app.
> The screenshots in the original Mac 128k user manual show the OS
> using the pre-release single-story ‘a’ before it was changed.


I double-checked using the (amazing) [classic Mac emulators at Infinite Mac](https://infinitemac.org/), and it turns out, Apple actually shipped System 1.0 with a version of Geneva with a single-story *a* glyph — but only in the 9-point version of Geneva. At 12 points (and larger), Geneva’s *a* was double-story. Here are screenshots of the Finder showing Geneva 9 in System 1.0 (with single-story *a*):

Geneva 9 — the eventual version, with a double-story *a* — is so intimately familiar to me that looking at those screenshots from System 1.0 makes me feel weird. It’s so clearly wrong. (What it feels like, to me, [is the original Palm OS](https://www.reddit.com/r/retrobattlestations/comments/1e5k4xb/a_few_years_ago_i_bought_a_beautiful_transparent/), from the one-bit Palm Pilot / Handspring Visor days. Palm’s small sans serif font was very Geneva-9-ish, but their single-story *a* was distinctive.)


Fox also posted a link to [Vintage Apple’s high-res scan of the amazing original Mac user manual](https://vintageapple.org/macmanuals/pdf/Macintosh_1984.pdf), which, because it had to go to press before the 1.0 software was finished, contains screenshots of a few icons [that changed by the time the original Mac was in customers’ hands](https://www.threads.com/@johncsiracusa/post/DJniWZ3NCCR). What a remarkably good user manual this is — everything from the typography, to the clarity and tone of its writing, to its comprehensiveness is exemplary.


Here’s where it really gets nutty though. Marcin Wichary — [whom you may recall](https://daringfireball.net/linked/2025/02/16/the-hardest-working-font-in-manhattan) from his recent remarkable deep dive on the Gorton typeface (“[The Hardest Working Font in Manhattan](https://aresluna.org/the-hardest-working-font-in-manhattan/)”), or from *[Shift Happens](https://shifthappens.site/)*, his encyclopedic book on the history of keyboards — [chimed in on Bluesky](https://bsky.app/profile/aresluna.org/post/3lozitikp5k2d) after observing that a few of the screenshots in that System 1.0 user manual show an early version of Chicago 12 with a single-story *a*. Seeing a single-story *a* in Chicago feels more blasphemous than that AI-generated image Trump tweeted of himself [as the new pope](https://www.cnn.com/2025/05/04/world/trump-ai-image-pope-intl-hnk).


One last note: I of course am not opposed to single-story *a*’s. Futura’s *a* is single-story, and [Futura](http://neverusefutura.com/), depending on my mood, might be my answer if asked to name my favorite typeface of all time. I just don’t particularly care for the alternate single-story *a* in San Francisco (Apple’s [modern San Francisco](https://developer.apple.com/fonts/), not [the one from 1984](https://daringfireball.net/misc/2025/05/system-1.0-san-francisco.png)), and to me it just gives an ever-so-slightly wrong — a little silly or unserious — vibe in the Notes app.
