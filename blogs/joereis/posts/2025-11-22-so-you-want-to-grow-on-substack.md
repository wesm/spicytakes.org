Every day, I see more and more people creating Substack accounts. Quite a few of them are moving from LinkedIn and similar platforms to Substack. I’m guessing many of these people want to grow their following and get more subscribers for their writing. Others might not want to be under the boot of a giant megacorp platform that’s undergoing some changes that aren’t beneficial to content creators. Optionality is always smart.


Substack has the reputation of being “a newsletter platform,” but that description is already outdated. What’s emerging here is something closer to a long-form social network, where writing is the currency rather than the short dopamine hits of TikTok, X, IG Reels, and YouTube Shorts. Longform writing should be slower, more deliberate, and more honest. Longform rewards depth, not noise. And for all those reasons, it might feel like a fresh start for people who want to think deeply in public.


Everyone starts from zero. When I moved to Substack a few years ago, I experienced the same shock most people do. I had a large following on other platforms and had a very popular book. But on Substack, I suddenly found myself starting over. I was a nobody. I remember watching the subscriber count crawl to 20 people and feeling genuinely excited. Yay for dopamine hits! But seriously, those 20 meant more to me than tens of thousands I had elsewhere. They were here for my writing, not the algorithm’s decision to feed it to them (that’s somewhat changed on Substack nowadays).


The first hard truth about Substack is that your audience might not follow you. Plan to start from zero just like everyone else. Your “follower count” from elsewhere doesn’t matter. The only thing that matters here is whether you can write something people want to return to. And trust me, there will be many days when you’re literally writing for the void. But if you’re a writer, you keep writing. If that sounds humbling, good. It should be. Humility is a far better foundation for growth than entitlement. However, the sad thing is 90% of people will quit here. They’ll publish a post or two, get very little engagement, then move on.


The second truth is that Substack is a long-form platform at its core, at leastfor now. Guess what you get to do? Yes, write longform. People sometimes underestimate how different writing long-form can be. Writing long-form isn’t the same as firing off a snappy post on LinkedIn or a clever soundbite on Twitter. Longform requires structured thinking. It requires patience. It requires sitting with an idea long enough for it to turn into something worth reading. And you’ll probably go through many iterations on an idea before you publish it. Embrace the grind. This means you’re getting better at thinking about the concept you’re noodling on and writing about.


Longform means that five hundred words is the smallest unit of meaning here. A thousand is better. Two thousand and more is where ideas begin to stretch their legs. Longform forces you to slow down, and slowing down forces clarity. It’s the difference between telling someone what you think and showing them how you feel.


But longform is lonely, too. This part almost nobody tells you, back to writing for the void. Starting to write publicly on Substack often feels like talking into an empty room, especially at first. You’ll hit publish, wait, refresh your screen a few times, and see nothing. No comments. Maybe two likes if you’re lucky. Perhaps not even that.


This is where most people quit. This is also where real writers separate from the tourists. And this is why I say that 90% of people quit. They probably quit too early. It is what it is.


Writer’s block is a real thing. I suffer from it, despite consistently writing every day. After I hit the 300-word mark, things start flowing. It might take me a bit to get there, sort of like warming up for exercise on a day when all you wanna do is lie on the couch. A neat trick I found to overcome writer’s block is voice-to-text. Especially when writing your zero or first draft, the point is to get your ideas on the page. So whether you’re writing on a keyboard or writing by speaking to a page, this accomplishes the same goal. In fact, I am writing this article by speaking into my phone as I’m walking around Paris this morning. You talk way faster than you type, so consider this a valid way to do your writing. Of course, you will need to edit using a keyboard and screen, but if voice-to-text helps you get some traction, go for it. I love it and use it all the time.


On a related note, youcanuse AI to generate your content. This is a tempting shortcut, but a dangerous one. While I find AI very useful as a sounding board and for editing my writing, I don’t use it to write my content wholesale. Writing is thinking, and if you’re not actively engaged in the writing process, you’re cheating yourself and your readers out of your true expression and voice. Again, AI is an absolutely fantastic assistant for brainstorming, ideation, editing, and other activities. But the active writing needs to come from you and your experience, not what some generic AI thinks you should write. Sadly, I expect Substack will beenshitifiedby AI-generated slop, which is disappointing. Ultimately, your readers and their engagement will be the ultimate test of whether you’re writing succeeds. Readers can see through AI slop. So, do yourself and your readers a favor and write something that truly expresses you, and only you.


Closing out, I’m excited to see a number of my friends, taking up writing as a medium to think through their ideas and express them in public. In the end, you’re doing this for yourself. Building an audience and engagement are side effects of writing things people want to read and reading stuff they care about. You can’t control this as much as you think. But what you can control is bringing yourself to your writing. Keep at it. Good luck and happy writing!
