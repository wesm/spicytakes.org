The other day, I had five separate incidents where people wanted to cold-pitch their product to me. One of these was with an old friend, and I’m happy to help him out. The others? Not so great. Then, a group of three people interrupted my conversation with a friend to pitch me on an idea, which, after a few minutes, left me very confused. I had to walk away. Another person who’s been messaging me nonstop accosted me and asked, “What will it take for you to look at my product?” With that approach, not a snowball’s chance in hell. These people are takers.


I’ve been thinking a lot about givers and takers. Most of what I do is free. I’ve spent many, many years paying it forward. Podcasting, writing, creating educational material, and stewarding communities. I’m no stranger to putting in a ton of work without charging upfront or expecting anything in return. Yes, I monetize some of this on the back end, but it’s not something I shove in people’s faces. If I tried to sell you something every five minutes, you’d tune out. And honestly, I’d feel gross about myself too. So I keep it simple: give away 90+% of what I create for free. My approach is1:many, instead of 1:1.


That’s very different from the approach I see a lot of people take, trying to mooch and monetize every step of the way. It doesn’t work. You’re competing with too much noise, too many options, and too little time. And if what you’re working on isn’t all that interesting (which, let’s be honest, most products aren’t), you’re doubling down on a losing strategy.


Ironically, the long game is sometimes the shortest path to success. Build your audience first. Build trust first. Then worry about monetization. That’s the giver mindset.


Every few months, I find myself makinga postabout how much I dislike unsolicited, lopsided pitches. Most of them are one-sided and transactional. They feel gross. And these solicitations never answer my question, which is “What’s in it for me?”


In the spirit of giving advice, here are some things I’ve found helpful for getting attention and building rapport, not just with me but with anyone else.

- Give more than you get. If I see you’re out in the community and doing positive things for the community, this will get a lot of points.
- Keep your pitch dead simple. Your pitch needs to be understandable in 30 seconds or less. My litmus test is that if your pitch is confusing and unclear within 30 seconds, it won’t resonate with your target audience.
- Show, don’t tell. A working demo is better than slides. Especially these days, when there are endless options to use AI to create a prototype, I’d rather see this than slides or a dogshit website.
- If I’m chatting with my friends, don’t butt in and interrupt us. Not only is that insanely rude, but I’ll find you incredibly annoying and tune you out.
- No, I won’t hop on a call with you. My time is extremely limited, and I don’t hand out meetings freely.
- If you still want to pitch your idea, please email me with the following. An email that clearly states what you’re doing, explains how this is a mutually beneficial exchange, includes a link to a demo, and/or a video of you using the product. Be clear on what you’re asking from me, and what I’m getting in return for investing my time and energy with you.
- If there’s no clear indication of mutual exchange, your message will be ignored. If you don’t hear from me, this is your answer.


It feels weird having to remind grown adults about simple things like courtesy, but here we are.


And to be clear: if we’ve built a relationship over time, I’m happy to help. I advise and invest in companies where I’ve gotten to know the founders and their teams. Relationships and trust are everything.


In the end, the people I say yes to are givers. They add more value than they take. And over time, that’s what wins.
