Last week during the Q&A part of my talk in San Francisco, a theme kept coming up over and over again, usually along the lines of “I want to do great work, but I don’t have enough time.” I spoke with an engineering lead who described his job as “adding more bubble wrap and duct tape” to his systems. The company he works at - a publicly traded FinTech company - has a lot of technical, data, and architectural debt carried over from when the company was a startup. No time was invested to refactor or rebuild things. Bubble wrap and duct tape. Two very popular tools in most engineers’ toolboxes these days.


These conversations got me thinking about Deep Work1and Delivery Work. Deep Work requires intense focus, discovery, creativity, and thinking. Design is an example of deep work. Whether you design a building, a system architecture, or a data model, you’rethinkingabout how different things interact, often considering second and third order effects. Deep Work takes time and patience.


Delivery Work involves delivering something (duh), like a product. TheScrum Guide states, “A product is a vehicle to deliver value. It has a clear boundary, known stakeholders, well-defined users or customers. A product could be a service, a physical product, or something more abstract.” A product might be something light, like a new slider widget in a mobile app, a color or border change in a CSS file, or a column added to a database. A product like a mobile phone or car might be far more complex. The main point of delivery work is delivering things that fulfill the needs for a product.


If you work in Sprints, you should read the Scrum Guide, since this is where the practice of Sprints originated. The guide is excellent and only 14 pages. When you understand the official Scrum framework, you can measure how effectively you use it in your team. Anecdotally, most teams only do Scrum by name. Often, it’s some version of Scrumfall, where you’re handed a pile of tickets from your product manager and the notion of iteration and review is ignored. Because “speed.” The technical debt backlog grows and is punted to a distant future where “we’ll get to it.” That day never arrives—more bubble wrap and duct tape. As an aside, it’s funny how people say they’re operating Lean and Agile, and yet commit one of the cardinal sins of waste - rework and backlogs. But that’s a rant for another week.


I often see both Deep and Delivery Work lumped into Sprints. A Sprint might have a ticket for conceptual data modeling, a notoriously Deep Work-heavy endeavor. Conceptual data modeling is an intense exercise in critical thinking, understanding the business and its vocabulary, and mapping this to a representation in data. This type of work doesn’t neatly fit into a two-week Sprint (these are the most common Sprint lengths I observe in the wild). Other examples are architecture or UX design, which require the same level of thoughtfulness and the occasional meanderings that discovery and design require.


This brings us back to the opening comment, “I want to do great work, but I don’t have enough time.” We need to stop shoehorning practices just because it’s convenient. This is lazy and shortsighted. It reminds me of an old Greek story called theBed of Procrustes, where Procrustes would kidnap people and stretch or chop their bodies to fit his bed. Gruesome for sure, and you’re probably wondering why he didn’t just have different beds for different people. But this is how we behave with practices like Sprints. Instead of determining the best way to do certain types of work, we force work into something convenient, whether it’s a good fit or not.


So how do you give yourself time to do great work? This is the crux many people are struggling with right now. If the incentive at the top is to move fast, that mentality will trickle down from leadership to practitioners. And unfortunately, most companies want to move fast, even at the expense of quality. This is part of the broader secular trend of making things cheap and disposable. And with AI, we’ll all be expected to be more “efficient” because AI is the productivity savior most of us didn’t ask for but get to exist with. Whereas back in the day, people took pride in the quality of their deliverables, nowadays one’s pride is associated with a clean burn down chart, working with an AI co-pilot that doesn’t hallucinate too often, and not getting fired this week.


Ideally, this is the best outcome if leadership is enlightened about the virtues of supporting Deep Work. Given the short termism in most leadership I’ve seen, this is wishful thinking. That could work if teams can find a way to shelter their Deep Work. Another option for Deep Work is adopting Kanban or Sprint Spikes. Kanban isn’t time-bounded, and Sprint Spikes are dedicated for research and other non-delivery work. Just make sure leadership and your team understand when Deep Work is being done, and set expectations that there’s inherent uncertainty with this type of work. There’s a chance that nothing comes of it. That’s ok.


Given the number of distressed and downtrodden practitioners I meet, I’m not exactly hopeful these suggestions will work. Change is hard, especially when you’re incentivized to move at breakneck speeds. You barely have enough time to question whether the way you’re working is workingforyou. However, the alternative is patching over problems with more bubble wrap and duct tape.


Have a wonderful weekend,


Joe
