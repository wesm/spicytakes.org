It's possible to be both a true believer in AI's power and a deep skeptic of the industry's hype. While I personally love AI, themarkets are telling a different story, and I’m reading more and more that the AI bubble is on the verge of popping. I’ve been through many bubbles, and this feels very familiar. Of course, the end of a bubble is always impossible to predict, and that’s something outside of my pay grade. And if I did have clear sight to when the bubble would pop, do you think I’d tell you? Nope.


Looking back atmy articlefrom last week, some people commented that they’re seeing a lot of utility from using AI. I fully agree and find that my selection of AI tools has been a massive boost to my productivity.


Here’s my main AI arsenal I use on a daily basis

- ChatGPT Pro
- Claude Max 20x
- Gemini Pro
- Cursor Pro
- Github Copilot


With the latter two, I’m on the fence about which one I want to keep. GitHub Copilot is half the price of Cursor, and they seem to be running neck and neck with each other every few weeks in terms of feature updates. Then there’s Claude Code, which is pretty damn amazing. I find myself running all three on a project, choosing between the three tools as I get throttled on one or the other. Claude Code does a lot of the heavy lifting, but Cursor and Copilot are also great at running tasks where I’d like the code visualization of the IDE.


I use ChatGPT and Gemini as my “business partners” and “editors” many times a day, constantly bouncing ideas off them. Their feedback is generally pretty spot on, and I’ll usually get some ideas I never considered. Claude is used to a lesser extent for non-coding work. That said, try using Claude Code to automate or streamline non-coding tasks. You’ll be surprised at its power.


To put it simply, I'm a happy, paying customer in the AI ecosystem. My workflow depends on these tools.


But does my personal ROI, or that of my peers, justify the eye-popping valuations and the market-wide frenzy? I wouldn’t give us too much credit here. We are early adopters benefiting from a VC-fueled boom, and our experience is not representative of the broader market.


This is slightly hilarious. I’ll give my circle the benefit of the doubt and trust that they’re data-driven. Yet we extrapolate our personal experiences to the broader market - chips, infrastructure, VCs, and their LPs. All of these are operating on a far different level than the average end-user (and benefactor) of these services. At the macro level, the investment in AI will pay off at some point, just as the investment in building the internet's infrastructure eventually led to the launch of countless billion-dollar companies. The question is whether that’s happening now, as the bubble continues to grow, or if that success is being deferred to a later time.


As I’ve repeatedly written, I’m very bullish on AI in the long term. My concern is that the story gets ahead of its skis and leads to another AI Winter. It won’t be the first time it has happened.  But given the Prisoner’s Dilemma of Bubbles, the only game to play is to keep pumping money into the bubble.


For now, the bubble is primarily centered on AI startups, frontier model bets, and ROI claims. What seems less bubble-ish for now are chips and infrastructure. As long as consumption can grow, more chips and infrastructure will be sold and built. But chips have a shelf life. If the demand drops, say, because ROI isn’t playing out, it would make sense that the stable areas will see a pullback. It only took one half-baked MIT study to cause a market drop. Imagine if the lack of ROI stories starts playing out more broadly over the next couple of quarters. NVIDIA will announce earnings very soon, and we’ll see how that goes.


AI is the only tale propping up the stock market right now. If it suddenly falls apart, and it’s not a great day for anyone. Of course, if the opposite happens and AI gets broad adoption and lots of wins in businesses, things rationalize. From what I can tell, most AI usage is of the free versions of stuff like ChatGPT. I hope more companies and individuals invest in the “better” AIs, agents gain more widespread adoption, and AI becomes more ingrained everywhere. But who knows? It’s super early days, especially when AI might probablybecome a utilityat some point.


Back to last week’s article, which focused on the story of AGI being right around the corner. The pursuit of AGI makes for a better story than the reality of enterprise automation, and right now, stories are what the market is buying. I doubt LLMs are what will take us to AGI. The bitter lesson being discovered is that theBitter Lessonworks until it doesn’t. If we could scale to better AI (or AGI) with the current approaches, wouldn’t we see it in GPT-5?


Ultimately, the landscape may resemble the aftermath of the dot-com era. The foundational technology will remain and become a utility, woven into everything we do. But the mountain of startups and sky-high valuations built on hype may not be so lucky. We’ve been here before.


In the meantime, I’ll keep enjoying my AI’s.
