*The vibe. Literally writing this on the plane, over the middle of nowhere.*


This is a bit of a different weekend newsletter blurb, and I’m writing this as I cross over Greenland on the way back to the US. Had a great trip in Helsinki, Finland, forHelsinki Data Week, a conference I accidentally “godfathered” a couple of years ago. Turns out, this conference (more like a week’s worth of different events) has rapidly become one of the best indie data events in the world. Shoutout to the Helsinki Data Mafia for the hard work and fantastic event.


Which brings me to the question I’m most frequently asked, “Why do you travel?” I haven’t really discussed this very much, so I’ll share my motivations here. The bottom line is that I am very curious about the world and our industry, and I feel one of the best ways to understand it better is to go out and meet the people doing the work. It’s one thing to read blog posts, watch videos, and see rumors on social media. It’s another thing to see things for yourself.


I am fortunate to travel the world and speak to audiences of leaders and practitioners. This often puts me in a position to have further discussions after my talk. It’s always fun, chatting with people, whether it’s on a long walk in the city, over a beer, or just in the hallways of the conference. I feel these are the most significant opportunities to learn about what other people are doing.


And a lot is happening right now. Whether it’s software or data engineering, everything is changing at warp speed. People are trying their best to make sense of the changes happening nearly daily. My message to audiences this Fall - AI has put everyone back at the starting line. I’ve rarely seen a more exciting time to create new ways of doing our work and craft. Don’t be a passive observer; be part of making this change happen. Invent new tools and new ways of working.


People in different geographies respond differently to my message. In the US, there’s a greater willingness to experiment and take risks. Even pockets of the US tend to move at different speeds. Overall, Europe moves a bit more slowly than the US. But universally, people feel something major is coming. Even at the climbing gym in Helsinki, I was chatting with two of the world’s best boulderers (a form of climbing) for over an hour about AI. They were two early 20s guys curious about what’s next, and how they should position their lives. As a former full-time climber, I could empathize with their lifestyle and hopes. Climbing is a fun sport, but you can’t climb at the highest level forever. And their views were fascinating because they’re not in our field. That conversation would never have happened if I had stayed put. I’m always trying to understand our world through the perspectives of people, particularly those outside our industry - random people, cab drivers, bartenders, baristas, etc.


Is travel easy? Definitely not. As Bill Inmon tells me, “travelling like you do will make you old.” He’s right. The jet lag sucks. Even worse is the time away from my family. Yesterday, my youngest son and his team won their football championship. I could only watch on FaceTime around midnight, then had to sleep for 3.5 hours to catch an early morning flight back to the US. I’m pretty fried right now, but writing this article in a dimly lit plane is somehow cathartic.


I’m also asked for travel tips, so here are a few big ones.

- Overpacking is easy, but you’ll only need half of what you think you’ll need. Be minimal. You can always buy things along the way.
- Don’t check in bags unless it’s necessary. It only adds extra time and risk if your bag is tragically lost.
- If you’re in the US, get Global Entry or TSA PreCheck. Especially if you get Global Entry, TSA PreCheck is thrown in. This will save you a ton of time getting through the lines.
- Sign up for an airline and hotel rewards program. I’m a Delta and Hyatt member, mainly because my hometown of Salt Lake City is a Delta hub, and Hyatt has a great rewards system. If you fly a lot like me, you’re almost always going to get a seat upgrade on your flight. You can translate your travels into free flights and hotels for later use.
- Meet locals and do local things. I have a lot of fun integrating myself into the local scene wherever I am. This is how you start seeing the hidden nuances of the world.
- For international travel, try to adjust yourself to the new time zone as quickly as possible. If you’re arriving at your destination in the morning, sleep. If you’re coming in the afternoon or evening, stay up. Your mind and body will thank you for the head start.


I’m sure there are more tips, but that’s the big ones I tell people. My goal is to optimize my time and energy to hang out with locals, see amazing things, give a talk, etc. If I’m ever in your area, please come and say hi.


For now, have a fun weekend,


Joe
