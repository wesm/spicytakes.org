I may have an unusual affinity for hardware, but [Steve Mann](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Mann) is in a class of his own. [He lives the hardware](http://query.nytimes.com/gst/fullpage.html?sec=technology&res=940CE0D71239F937A25750C0A9649C8B63). Steve Mann may be the world’s original cyborg.


> Steve Mann, an engineering professor at the University of Toronto, has lived as a cyborg for more than 20 years, wearing a web of wires, computers and electronic sensors that are designed to augment his memory, enhance his vision and keep tabs on his vital signs.


Mr. Mann has been exploring the frontiers of wearable computers since 1980.


![](https://blog.codinghorror.com/content/images/2025/05/image-512.png)


Steve is [evidently at version 7](http://www.wearcam.org/wearhow/node1.html) of his wearable computer. I’m surprised he hasn’t updated the rig since 1999.


There was a flurry of interest in his work in 2001, which coincided with a [book](http://wearcam.org/cyborg.htm) and a [movie](http://www.imdb.com/title/tt0301145/) made about Steve. There’s a companion [article on LinuxDevices](https://web.archive.org/web/20070816222703/http://www.linuxdevices.com/articles/AT8144626322.html) describing how to build a similar rig from 2001, but it feels ancient by today’s standards.


It’s hard to tell what’s currently happening in the field of wearable computing. [Steve’s personal web page](http://wearcam.org/) is a shambles; nothing is dated. The [MIT wearable computing page](http://www.media.mit.edu/wearables/) hasn’t been updated since 2005, and the [Wearable Computing home](https://web.archive.org/web/20070822210037/http://about.eyetap.org/index.shtml) appears to be in a state of limbo. At least [Andy’s wearable computing resource](https://web.archive.org/web/20071017041245/http://www.redwoodhouse.com/wearable/) looks like it’s being updated on a regular basis.


Somehow, I thought the cyborgs would have a more compelling web presence.
