Hard disk space, like CPU speed, isn’t increasing as fast [as it used to](https://web.archive.org/web/20050207102143/http://www.digit-life.com/articles2/digests/hdd2k4.html):


![](https://blog.codinghorror.com/content/images/2025/05/image-44.png)


Still, 400gb drives can be had, and it’s not difficult to build a terabyte array if you need to. Which begs the question – where does all that hard drive space go?


Two tools I use to demystify the “gee, I had 50gb free yesterday” conundrum are [overdisk](https://web.archive.org/web/20050405222050/http://users.forthnet.gr/pat/efotinis/programs/overdisk.html):


![](https://blog.codinghorror.com/content/images/2025/05/image-45.png)


... and [Treesize](https://www.jam-software.com/treesize_free):


![](https://blog.codinghorror.com/content/images/2025/05/image-46.png)


Stuart Dootson also pointed out [spacemonger](https://web.archive.org/web/20050123033744/http://www.werkema.com/software/spacemonger.html), another graphical representation:


![spacemonger screenshot](https://blog.codinghorror.com/content/images/uploads/2005/01/6a0120a85dcdae970b0128776fbe5d970c-pi.png)


I like the [Tufte-esque](http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0961392118) visual style of overdisk, but I’m not convinced the information is any more useful when presented in that format. I run Treesize on servers, too, and I’d like to find a command-line equivalent of Treesize if anyone knows of one.
