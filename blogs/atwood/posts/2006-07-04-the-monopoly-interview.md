Reginald Braithwaite’s [favorite interview question](http://weblog.raganwald.com/2006/06/my-favourite-interview-question.html) is an offbeat one: **sketch out a software design to referee the game **[**Monopoly**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monopoly_(game)).*


I think it’s a valid design exercise which neatly skirts the [puzzle question trap](http://www.techinterview.org/). But more importantly, **it’s fun**.


Interviews are a terror for the interviewee. And they’re stressful for the interviewer, too. A design exercise centered around a salt-of-the-earth game like Monopoly is a great way to put both parties at ease. Lots of people have played Monopoly at some point, so you have a nice, common base of familiarity to work with.


![](https://blog.codinghorror.com/content/images/2025/07/monopoly-board-1935.jpg)


Anything is better than the “how would you write a routine to copy a file” [interview question](https://blog.codinghorror.com/snappy-answers-to-stupid-programming-questions/), but any company that asks an entertaining and useful interview question like this is already a winner in my book.


But what I love most about the Monopoly question is how it sucks me in. Maybe it’s because I’m a gamer at heart, but my mind immediately starts racing through all the different possibilities. It’s a little embarrassing to admit, but I’d love nothing more than to sit in a room with another programmer and hash this problem out.


Because it’s *fun*.


And isn’t programming [supposed to be fun?](https://blog.codinghorror.com/our-programs-are-fun-to-use/)
