Laptop magazine’s [Attack of the 20-inch Notebook](https://web.archive.org/web/20061018061642/http://laptopmag.com/Features/Attack-of-the-20-inch-Notebook.htm) is a tongue-in-cheek look at using the Dell XPS M2010 as a portable system in a few different locations.


![](https://blog.codinghorror.com/content/images/2025/05/image-366.png)


Hilarity ensued. For context, here are the relevant specs of this semi-portable concept system:

- 20" LCD
- full-size bluetooth keyboard (with numeric pad)
- 16.75" h x 19.25" w x 3" d
- 20.3 pounds with AC adapter


Shades of the original 28 pound [Compaq Portable](https://web.archive.org/web/20061019071043/http://www.old-computers.com/MUSEUM/computer.asp?st=1&c=547), although the 9" text-mode monochrome monitor and 4.77MHz 8088 aren’t nearly as impressive.


Although the XPS M2010 is an extreme case, I never quite understood the fascination of those giant 10+ pound desktop replacement laptops. You can even buy battery-less laptops that must be plugged in – [so-called “Mobile PCs.”](http://arstechnica.com/news.ars/post/20020715-1987.html) Isn’t *portability* the whole point of a notebook computer?


I adored my three pound Dell Inspiron 300; travelling with it was effortless. I compromised a bit for maximum Vista performance with [my 5.5 pound Asus W3J](https://blog.codinghorror.com/asus-w3j-laptop-review/), which is a great machine, but at nearly double the weight it’s also a more noticeable burden. That’s absolutely as large as I’m willing to go for a laptop. I’d much rather be using an ultraportable like the [2.2 pound Samsung Q2010](https://web.archive.org/web/20061017112729/http://reviews.cnet.com/Fujitsu_LifeBook_Q2010/4505-3121_7-31959337.html#more) or the [3 pound Dell D420](https://web.archive.org/web/20061018062646/http://www.dell.com/content/products/features.aspx/latit_d420?c=us&cs=04&l=en&s=bsd).
