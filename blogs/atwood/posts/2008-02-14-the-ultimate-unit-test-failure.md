We programmers are [obsessive by nature](https://blog.codinghorror.com/software-developers-and-aspergers-syndrome/). But I often get frustrated with the depth of our obsession over things like code coverage. Unit testing and code coverage are [good](https://blog.codinghorror.com/i-pity-the-fool-who-doesnt-write-unit-tests/) [things](https://blog.codinghorror.com/good-test-bad-test/). But perfectly executed code coverage doesn’t mean users will use your program. Or that it’s even *worth* using in the first place. **When users can’t figure out how to use your app, when users **[**pass over your app**](https://blog.codinghorror.com/designing-for-informavores-or-why-users-behave-like-animals-online/)** in favor of something easier or simpler to use, *that’s* the ultimate unit test failure.** *That’s* the problem you should be trying to solve.


I want to run up to my fellow programmers and physically shake them: think bigger!


A perfect example of *thinking bigger* is Alan Cooper’s [Interaction 08](http://interaction08.ixda.org/) keynote, **An Insurgency of Quality**.


![](https://blog.codinghorror.com/content/images/2025/04/image-211.png)


There’s a [transcript of his keynote](https://web.archive.org/web/20080222122000/http://ajaxian.com/archives/interaction08-ixds-in-savannah-alan-cooper) available or you can view a [video of his keynote speech](https://ixda.org/video/an-insurgency-of-quality) with the slides in place.


Alan is a well known interaction designer , and the author of several classic books in the field, such as [About Face](https://blog.codinghorror.com/the-three-faces-of-about-face/), and a few others that are on my [recommended reading list](https://blog.codinghorror.com/recommended-reading-for-developers/). In the Q&A after the talk, he had this to say:

kg-card-begin: html

> “We are not very important because we don’t cut code.” (A boo and hiss from the audience.) In the world of high-technology, if you cut code, you control things. It’s the power to destroy the spice, it’s the power to control the spice. It’s not a fine kind of control: it’s bruce-force kind of things. [Interaction designers are] largely marginalized. We’re constantly asking for permission from the folks who shouldn’t be in a position to grant permission. We should be working with business folks and marshalling the technology to meet the solutions to business problems.
>     But when it comes time to marshal the solution to the problems, we find ourselves slamming into this kind of Stay-Puft Marshmallow Man of software development. 
> **We don’t need to change interaction design; we need to re-orient organizations to build things right.** When we come to programmers and say, “Look at the people I’ve talked to; look at the personas I’ve created” and present them with research, programmers understand that, and that’s how we will influence.

kg-card-end: html

It pains me to hear that **Cooper considers most programmers twenty-story marshmallow barriers to good interaction design**. Please don’t be one of those programmers. Learn about the science of interaction design. Pick up a copy of [Don’t Make Me Think](https://blog.codinghorror.com/dont-make-me-think-second-edition/) as an introduction if you haven’t already. There’s a reason this book is at the top of my recommended reading list. Keep your unit testing and code coverage in perspective – the *ultimate* unit test is whether or not users want to use your application. All the other tests you write are totally irrelevant until you can get that one to pass.
