[In-N-Out Burger](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/In-N-Out_Burger) is a fast food institution here in California. Part of their appeal, I think, is their radically simplified menu.


![In-n-Out Burger menu](https://blog.codinghorror.com/content/images/uploads/2007/09/6a0120a85dcdae970b0120a86d9487970b-pi.jpg)


Instead of forcing customers to process a complex menu with a hundred choices, In-N-Out [got real](http://gettingreal.37signals.com/) and pared it down to what really matters: **a burger, fries, and a drink**. It’s a limited experience, but it’s also a tightly focused one. The menu may be small, but you can still customize it – for advanced In-N-Out customers, there’s the secret menu.


Most software lacks the discipline to present the tightly controlled user experience of the In-N-Out menu. Instead, we befuddle users with the software equivalent of a [fourteen-course restaurant menu](http://www.dansdata.com/gz073.htm). Software developers lapse into [complexity by default](https://blog.codinghorror.com/simplicity-as-a-force/), because it’s the path of least resistance.


![notetab options dialog](https://blog.codinghorror.com/content/images/uploads/2007/09/6a0120a85dcdae970b0120a86d9490970b-pi.png)


Why can’t we build software the In-N-Out way, and **keep the menu simple**? Stop trying to do everything. Don’t [make users think](https://blog.codinghorror.com/dont-make-me-think-second-edition/). Focus on doing a few things exceptionally well, and leave the giant, confusing menu of options for your competitors.
