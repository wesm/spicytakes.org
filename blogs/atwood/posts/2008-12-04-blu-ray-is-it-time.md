I’ve been monitoring the progress of high-definition video playback on the PC for quite a while now:

- [Next-Gen DVD: Are Those Additional Pixels Worth Your Money?](https://blog.codinghorror.com/next-gen-dvd-are-those-additional-pixels-worth-your-money/)
- [High-Definition Video on the PC](https://blog.codinghorror.com/high-definition-video-on-the-pc/)
- [Is Your PC Capable of Hi-Def?](https://blog.codinghorror.com/is-your-pc-capable-of-hi-def/)
- [Will Your Next Computer Monitor Be a HDTV?](https://blog.codinghorror.com/will-your-next-computer-monitor-be-a-hdtv/)


It’s been almost two years since I wrote that series, and I think we’re **dangerously close to viable high definition video playback on typical, mainstream PCs**. One metric I follow closely is the price of the hardware, and OEM Blu-Ray drives are [now only $99 shipped](http://www.newegg.com/Product/Product.aspx?Item=N82E16827136154).


![](https://blog.codinghorror.com/content/images/2025/04/image-234.png)


This drive is a DVD burner, in addition to playing HD-DVDs, Blu-Ray, and obviously DVDs – and it also has very positive customer reviews. I couldn’t resist, so I bought one.


I have no need for a standalone Blu-Ray player, but a cursory look tells me those are down to around $250 for decent models. And then of course there’s always the PlayStation 3 option.


It’s a shame OS X and Vista don’t natively support HD playback of any kind (although Vista does include some copy protection mechanisms specific to high-definition video playback, which was the source of [great hue and outrage](http://www.cs.auckland.ac.nz/~pgut001/pubs/vista_cost.html)). When you pair this $99 drive with some third party playback software like PowerDVD HD or WinDVD HD, you’re set.


I’m particularly interested in high definition PC playback because the [home theater ](https://blog.codinghorror.com/building-your-own-home-theater-pc/)[PC](https://blog.codinghorror.com/building-your-own-home-theater-pc/) I recently built is more than capable:

- Built in HDMI out (on the motherboard)
- Onboard video that [supports H.264 acceleration](https://web.archive.org/web/20081216020342/http://techreport.com/articles.x/14261/9)
- A modern dual-core CPU


Also, I finally own a true 1920 x 1080 HDTV now – yes, you can all stop making fun of me for using a creaky old brass and steam powered 852 x 480 EDTV – so all the pieces are now in place for me to adopt Blu-Ray. I switched my Netflix account over to Blu-Ray this morning.


I’m not quite a high definition video early adopter, but I’m still on the leading edge of the curve. **Funny how technology cycles repeat themselves.** I distinctly recall being an early adopter of DVDs back in 1998, almost exactly 10 years ago. The 720 x 480 resolution and Dolby Digital sound seemed so impressive back then. I remember marveling at the fancy interactive menus on the Austin Powers DVD. Of course, DVD quality is [pretty pedestrian by today’s standards](https://blog.codinghorror.com/is-dvd-the-new-vhs/). We’ve *almost* gotten to the point where DVD-level video quality is available worldwide in a typical web browser, not necessarily [through YouTube](https://blog.codinghorror.com/did-youtube-cut-the-gordian-knot-of-video-codecs/), but [through Vimeo](http://www.vimeo.com/highdef) and other alternatives.


With that in mind, I wonder how quaint Blu-Ray will seem in 2018?
