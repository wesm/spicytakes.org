As the old adage goes:


> *Good programmers write good code; great programmers steal great code.*


This is definitely true, mostly because great programmers have learned to do some research before writing anything at all. However, even great programmers tend to be absolutely [terrible at graphic design](https://asp-blogs.azurewebsites.net/jeff/254664), even though the solution is exactly the same: **never design what you can steal**.


A prime candidate for theft is this [cheeky study in redesign](https://web.archive.org/web/20041206195540/http://www.designbyfire.com/000094.html). It works as a funny parody of *Queer Eye for the Straight Guy*, but best of all, it targets [a page from Jakob Nielsen](https://web.archive.org/web/20050109025728/http://www.useit.com/alertbox/20040510.html) – a usability guru, who often gets a black eye for his intentionally low-tech design layouts.


I was rather skeptical of this effort at first. As a developer, **I sympathize with crappy layouts**. It’s what I do! I make really crappy, basic HTML layouts. Then I fool myself into believing crappy, basic HTML layout is a good thing because it’s, you know, lightweight. Like Google! But these guys made me a believer. Their redesigns were a big improvement over the original, minimalist Nielsen layout:

1. [The original Nielsen Alertbox](https://web.archive.org/web/20050109025728/http://www.useit.com/alertbox/20040510.html)
2. [Rewrite of the text](https://web.archive.org/web/20060112041831/http://www.designbyfire.com/deye_web/straight_text.htm)
3. [Redesign of the web page, with illustrations](https://web.archive.org/web/20050207013212/http://www.designbyfire.com/deye_web/alertbox.htm)

Anyway, it was heartening to see how a few simple design changes could dramatically improve the basic HTML I regularly churn out. And if that doesn’t convince you...
