Extreme Programming (XP) is a software development methodology
  developed primarily by [Kent
  Beck](https://twitter.com/KentBeck). XP was one of the first [agile methods](https://martinfowler.com/articles/newMethodology.html), indeed XP was the
  dominant agile method in the late 90s and early 00s before Scrum
  became dominant as the noughties passed. Many people (including
  myself) consider XP to be the primary catalyst that got attention to
  agile methods, and superior to Scrum as a base for starting out in
  agile development.


Jim Shore's description of Agile Software Development is heavily based on
    the principles of Extreme Programming, and is my first choice as a guide to
    learning the XP approach.


Kent developed XP over the course of his consulting to Smalltalk
  projects in the late 80s and early 90s. The full set of practices
  that came to be known as XP were first used together in the
  [C3](https://martinfowler.com/bliki/C3.html) project (where I worked with Kent and learned about
  it). The name “Extreme Programming” came later as the approach was
  described, first informally on the [WikiWikiWeb](http://c2.com/cgi/wiki?WikiWikiWeb) and then later in a series
  of books. Various teams took the description in the WikiWikiWeb and
  implemented XP themselves, thus replicating the methodology and
  showing it could be used outside its original home.


Kent describes XP by a progression of ideas from broad and
  abstract values through principles, to concrete practices - a
  progression that I find useful to apply in many other contexts. It
  popularized many practices that have since been widely used in
  software development, including: [continuous
  integration](https://martinfowler.com/articles/continuousIntegration.html), [refactoring](http://refactoring.com/),
  [TestDrivenDevelopment](https://martinfowler.com/bliki/TestDrivenDevelopment.html), and agile planning. I particularly
  like its combination of technical and management practices, which
  make it a good fit for reaching the delivering zone of [agile fluency](https://martinfowler.com/articles/agileFluency.html) 1.

## Further Reading


The definitive description of Extreme Programming is [Kent’s
    white book](https://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0321278658/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&camp=1789&creative=9325&creativeASIN=0321278658&linkCode=as2&tag=martinfowlerc-20). (Beware that many of the early practitioners of XP
    learned the approach using the first edition, and there’s quite a few differences.)


Although the White Book is the best definition of XP, these days I
    recommend James Shore’s [The Art of Agile Development](https://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1492080691/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&camp=1789&creative=9325&creativeASIN=1492080691&linkCode=as2&tag=martinfowlerc-20) as a guide to
    learning the XP approach. Although Shore casts his net wider than Kent’s
    work on XP, his book is firmly grounded on XP’s key features.
