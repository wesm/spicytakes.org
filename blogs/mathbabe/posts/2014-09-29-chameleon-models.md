[Here’s an interesting paper](http://www.gsb.stanford.edu/gsb-cmis/gsb-cmis-download-doc/359071) I’m reading this morning (hat tip Suresh Naidu) entitled [*Chameleons: The Misuse of Theoretical Models in Finance and Economics*](http://www.gsb.stanford.edu/gsb-cmis/gsb-cmis-download-doc/359071) written by [Paul Pfleiderer](http://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/faculty/paul-pfleiderer). The paper introduces the useful concept of chameleon models, defined in the following diagram:


[Pfleiderer](http://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/faculty/paul-pfleiderer) provides some examples of chameleon models, and also takes on the [Milton Friedman argument that we shouldn’t judge a model by its assumptions but rather by its predictions](http://unlearningeconomics.wordpress.com/2013/02/08/against-friedman-why-assumptions-matter/) (personally I think this is largely dependent on the way a model is used; the larger the stakes, the more the assumptions matter).


I like the term, and I think I might use it. I also like the point he makes that it’s really about usage. Most models are harmless until they are used as political weapons. Even the value-added teacher model could be used to identify school systems that need support, although in the current climate of distorted data due to teaching to the test and cheating, I think the signal is probably very slight.
