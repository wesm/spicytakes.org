**Today**


Today I’m super excited to go to the [opening launch party of danah boyd’s Data and Society](http://www.datasociety.net/updates/2014/09/september-30-updates/). Data and Society has a bunch of cool initiatives but I’m particularly interested in their [Council for Big Data, Ethics, and Society](http://www.datasociety.net/initiatives/council-for-big-data-ethics-and-society/). They were the people that helped make the [Podesta Report on Big Data](https://mathbabe.org/2014/05/07/inside-the-podesta-report-civil-rights-principles-of-big-data/) as good as it was. There will be a mini-conference this afternoon I’m looking forward to very much. Brilliant folks doing great work and talking to each other across disciplinary lines, can’t get enough of that stuff.

**December 12th**


In mid-December I’m on a panel myself at the [*Fairness, Accountability, and Transparency in Machine Learning *Conference in Montreal](http://fatml.org/index.html). This conference seems to directly take up the call of the Podesta Report I mentioned above, and seeks to provide further research into the dangers of “encoding discrimination in automated decisions”. Amazing! So glad this is happening and that I get to be part of it. Here are some questions that will be taken up at this one-day conference (more information [here](http://fatml.org/index.html)):

- How can we achieve high classification accuracy while eliminating discriminatory biases? What are meaningful formal fairness properties?
- How can we design expressive yet easily interpretable classifiers?
- Can we ensure that a classifier remains accurate even if the statistical signal it relies on is exposed to public scrutiny?
- Are there practical methods to test existing classifiers for compliance with a policy?
