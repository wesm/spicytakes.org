Today I read [this article](https://www.jacobinmag.com/2014/07/the-charter-school-profiteers/) written by Allie Gross (hat tip Suresh Naidu), a former [*Teach for America*](http://www.teachforamerica.org/) teacher whose former idealism has long been replaced by her experiences in the reality of education in this country. Her article is entitled *[The Charter School Profiteers](https://www.jacobinmag.com/2014/07/the-charter-school-profiteers/).*


It’s really important, and really well written, and just one of the articles in the online magazine *[Jacobin](https://www.jacobinmag.com/)* that I urge you to read and to subscribe to. In fact that article is part of a series ([here’s another](https://www.jacobinmag.com/2014/07/no-excuses-in-new-orleans/) which focuses on charter schools in New Orleans) and it comes with a booklet called *[Class Action: An Activist Teacher’s Handbook](https://www.jacobinmag.com/2014/02/class-action-an-activist-teachers-handbook/).* I just ordered a couple of hard copies.


I’d really like you to read [the article](https://www.jacobinmag.com/2014/07/the-charter-school-profiteers/), but as a teaser here’s one excerpt, a rant which she completely backs up with facts on the ground:


> You haven’t heard of [Odeo](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odeo), the failed podcast company the Twitter founders initially worked on? Probably not a big deal. You haven’t heard about the failed education ventures of the person now running your district? Probably a bigger deal.
> When we welcome schools that lack democratic accountability (charter school boards are appointed, not elected), when we allow public dollars to be used by those with a bottom line (such as the for-profit management companies that proliferate in Michigan), we open doors for opportunism and corruption. Even worse, it’s all justified under a banner of concern for poor public school students’ well-being.
> While these issues of corruption and mismanagement existed before, we should be wary of any education reformer who claims that creating an education marketplace is the key to fixing the ills of DPS or any large city’s struggling schools. Letting parents pick from a variety of schools does not weed out corruption. And the lax laws and lack of accountability can actually exacerbate the socioeconomic ills we’re trying to root out.
