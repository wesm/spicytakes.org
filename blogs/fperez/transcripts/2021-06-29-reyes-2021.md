### Gravitational Waves and Open Science

As an example, the 2017 Nobel Prize in physics was awarded to those who led a very large team who made the first observation of gravitational waves. Basically what Einstein said was space and time can ripple and those ripples can travel like waves on the surface of a pond — but it's the four-dimensional fabric of spacetime itself where those ripples are traveling. If two big things like two black holes slam into one another, they're going to make a lot of ripples in that lake.

In 2015, the LIGO collaboration published a paper whose first figure is probably going to be one of the most cited papers in the history of physics. That figure was made all with NumPy, with SciPy, with Matplotlib — the color maps were developed at UC Berkeley. All of this was done with the tools that 10, 15 years earlier we were struggling to create, against many who said it was a waste of our time, a waste of our career, that no one would fund it. And now in 2015, these publications are being made exactly with these tools.

The team from the LIGO collaboration actually posted their analysis so that anyone can run it in a Jupyter Notebook through the web without installing anything. You can actually hear the sound of two black holes, 1.3 billion light years away, shedding something on the order of three solar masses in the form of pure energy that traveled across spacetime. That rise in frequency comes from the fact that as the two black holes spiral into one another faster and faster, the frequency of the ripple increases. The fact that anyone can run this analysis with Jupyter in your web browser without installing anything is a wonderful example of why this kind of open collaborative science is valuable.

I credit a lot the video series Cosmos, that documentary by Carl Sagan, which was hugely influential in my teenage years, showing me the discovery of science and the unifying elegance of physics as a beautiful way of looking at the world.

The question is, with such a weird career path, was that interest justified? And do I have any regrets? I don't. Physics is a wonderful discipline that combines theoretical rigor, mathematical foundations, and a very practical, hands-on way of looking at the world. In physics, we use theory, mathematics, and computing, but we use them to build things that have to work in the real world. Our models have to meet the harsh reality of nature. That mix of theoretical depth, technical sophistication, and practicality is wonderful.

It gives you a lens to think about the world. I may not be doing as much physics research these days, though I am coming back with some of our geoscience and climate work. I'm certainly not doing QCD or field theory, but those principles still help me organize my thinking. You can't argue with conservation of energy. You can't argue with the laws of gravity. Understanding how those building blocks work together is a great foundation for a lot of other things. I still find physics to be a wonderful starting point for many careers, regardless of whether you do end up being a physicist.

### How Does Sharing Jupyter Notebooks Work?

**Question from Tomas:** How does sharing your Jupyter notebook with others work? Do you need a server to let the shared notebooks run on?

**Fernando:** Great question. There are three levels.

The first is called NBViewer. NBViewer is a simple way to share Jupyter notebooks where you put either a link to a notebook or a link to a GitHub repository that has notebooks. You get a rendered version of that notebook — they can't run anything, but they could download it. It's a handy way, without having to do anything, as long as it's a notebook somewhere online, to share a rendered view.

The second is Binder. Binder lets you put down the name of a GitHub repository, and as long as that repository explicitly says which libraries are needed to run the code, Binder automatically collects those dependencies, clones everything, puts it in a Docker container, and deploys it in the cloud. All you have to do is begin hitting Shift-Enter and you're actually running the code. I showed this with the LIGO example — you can run the analysis against the real LIGO data without installing anything, because it's running in a Docker container with that repository and dependencies in the cloud.

The third is the real-time collaborative editing capabilities of JupyterLab, our modern user interface for notebooks. It includes real-time Google Docs-style editing. This is very new but coming rapidly — it lets you share notebooks that you can co-edit live. This is obviously very valuable for both scientific collaborations and teaching. We actually tried a live demo during the talk, and Thomas was able to join and type code in the shared notebook. So those are the three levels: with NBViewer you can share view links, with Binder you can share executable copies, and with this new machinery you'll be able to share real live collaboration.

### Getting Started with Scientific Computing

**Question from Danielle:** As someone who is less experienced in data science without a deep programming background, what tools would you recommend for learning Python and scientific computing?

**Fernando:** There's a lot of material for new interested scientists. I want to flag Project Pythia, a newly funded project from the National Science Foundation developing resources for geoscientists interested in orienting themselves. Sometimes the problem is more "where do I start?" and Project Pythia is trying to solve that for newcomers to earth science. Our project Jupyter Meets the Earth is also adding explanatory links and we just ran a workshop aimed at answering this question, with longer versions of the videos to be uploaded over the coming weeks.

The other recommendation: check out Data 8 at data8.org. Our Data 8 course is actually a pretty good place to start — it's very introductory but gets you going with real-world data examples. It's all open source, set up for your own use, and the videos and the whole course are available online. It's a good starting point before launching into the bigger waters of what's available on Google.
